# Jean-Luc Brunel, MC2 Model Management, and the Epstein Trafficking Pipeline

---

## I. JEAN-LUC BRUNEL — BACKGROUND

### The Paris Modelling World

Jean-Luc Brunel (1946–2022) was a French model scout and agency manager who operated at the upper echelons of the international fashion industry for more than four decades. Born in Paris, Brunel entered the modelling business in the early 1970s, rapidly establishing himself as a talent scout with an eye for young women who could be marketed to the haute couture and commercial fashion worlds. Over the course of his career, Brunel was associated with several prominent agencies:

- **Karin Models (Paris)**: Brunel co-founded and ran Karin Models in Paris in the late 1970s and 1980s. The agency became a significant player in the Parisian fashion scene, scouting models from across Europe and South America and placing them with major fashion houses and photographers (Gross, 1995). [CORROBORATED]

- **Europlanning**: Brunel also operated under the Europlanning banner, a scouting and placement operation that specialized in recruiting young women from Brazil, Eastern Europe, and Scandinavia and bringing them to Paris on the promise of modelling careers (Gross, 1995; BBC News, 2020). [CORROBORATED]

- **Elite Model Management**: Though not a founder, Brunel maintained close ties with John Casablancas and the Elite agency ecosystem throughout the 1980s and early 1990s. These relationships gave him access to a pipeline of aspiring models and to the social milieu of fashion industry power brokers (Gross, 1995). [CORROBORATED]

Brunel's operational specialty was the discovery and initial placement of very young women — often aged 14 to 18 — whom he would identify in their home countries, promise international careers, and then manage through the earliest and most vulnerable phases of their modelling work. This role — the "discoverer" and "booker" of new talent — placed Brunel in a position of extraordinary power over young women who were typically far from home, unfamiliar with the language, financially dependent on their agent, and often in the country on visas tied directly to the agency's sponsorship.

### The 1980s Allegations

Long before Brunel's name became linked to Jeffrey Epstein, he was the subject of serious and well-documented allegations of sexual abuse and exploitation within the modelling industry.

- **The 60 Minutes Investigation (1988)**: In a landmark segment, CBS's *60 Minutes* broadcast an investigation into sexual exploitation in the Paris modelling world. Multiple young models described being drugged and sexually assaulted by agents and photographers. Brunel was specifically named. Several models alleged that Brunel had assaulted them or facilitated their assault by others. One former model, speaking on camera, described being drugged at a dinner arranged by Brunel (CBS News, 1988). [CORROBORATED — multiple on-camera testimonies; corroborated by subsequent accounts]

- **Carré Otis's Memoir**: American model and actress Carré Otis detailed her experiences in the Paris modelling world in her 2011 memoir *Beauty, Disrupted*. Otis described encountering Brunel as a teenager in the 1980s and recounted a pattern of predatory behavior — including being pressured into sexual situations by agents and associates in the Paris scene. While Otis's memoir does not name Brunel as a direct assailant in every instance, she describes a culture in which agents like Brunel wielded unchecked power over young models, and her account is consistent with the broader pattern documented by *60 Minutes* and investigative journalist Michael Gross (Otis, 2011). [SINGLE SOURCE — memoir; consistent with pattern evidence]

- **Michael Gross's** ***Model: The Ugly Business of Beautiful Women*** **(1995)**: Fashion journalist Michael Gross's seminal investigation of the modelling industry devoted significant attention to Brunel. Gross documented dozens of allegations against Brunel from models, former employees, and industry insiders — allegations ranging from unwanted sexual advances to outright rape. Gross wrote that "Jean-Luc Brunel's name comes up over and over again" in accounts of exploitation, and quoted multiple named and unnamed sources describing Brunel as a serial predator (Gross, 1995, pp. 336–358). [CORROBORATED — multiple independent sources across years]

### Why Brunel Retained Prominence

The most damning question in Brunel's pre-Epstein biography is not whether allegations existed — they were public, broadcast on American network television, and documented in a bestselling book — but why they produced no meaningful consequences. Several structural factors explain Brunel's continued operation:

1. **The industry's economic incentives**: Brunel was a revenue generator. The agencies and fashion houses that profited from his "discoveries" had powerful financial reasons to overlook or minimize complaints. A model scout who reliably delivered commercially viable young women was too valuable to discard over allegations that the industry preferred to treat as gossip (Gross, 1995). [CORROBORATED]

2. **The vulnerability of accusers**: The models who might have pursued legal claims against Brunel were, almost without exception, young, foreign, financially precarious, and dependent on the good will of the very industry figures they would need to accuse. Many were in France on work visas; an accusation against a powerful agent could mean the end of a career and, in practical terms, deportation (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

3. **Jurisdictional complexity**: Brunel operated across multiple countries — France, the United States, Brazil, Israel — making coordinated law enforcement action difficult. Allegations that arose in Paris involved French legal standards and procedures that, at the time, offered limited protections for sexual assault victims and imposed high evidentiary burdens (Le Monde, 2020). [CORROBORATED]

4. **Fashion journalism's complicity**: The fashion press, dependent on access to designers, agencies, and industry events, had strong incentives not to pursue investigative reporting on industry predators. The *60 Minutes* segment and Gross's book were produced by journalists outside the fashion media ecosystem; within it, Brunel's behavior was widely known but rarely, if ever, the subject of sustained reporting (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

### Meeting Epstein

The precise circumstances and date of the Brunel-Epstein introduction remain partially obscured. The most credible accounts place the initial meeting in the late 1980s or early 1990s, likely facilitated through mutual social connections in New York and Paris:

- **Virginia Giuffre's testimony** places Ghislaine Maxwell as a key intermediary, stating that Maxwell introduced Brunel into the Epstein circle and that Brunel and Epstein's relationship was well established by the late 1990s (Giuffre v. Maxwell, Deposition, 2016). [CORROBORATED — deposition testimony; consistent with documentary evidence]

- **Financial records** introduced at the Maxwell trial demonstrate that by the early 2000s, Epstein was providing substantial financial support to Brunel, including direct funding for the founding of MC2 Model Management (United States v. Maxwell, Trial Exhibit GX-351, 2021). [CONFIRMED]

- **Brunel's own communications**, recovered from Epstein's records, show regular contact — phone messages, scheduling, and travel coordination — that indicates a close, ongoing relationship from at least the mid-1990s onward. One document from Epstein's files lists Brunel as having called Epstein's Palm Beach residence on dozens of occasions (Recarey Police Report, Palm Beach PD, 2005). [CONFIRMED — law enforcement records]

The Brunel-Epstein relationship was, from its inception, characterized by a mutual exchange: Brunel provided access to a steady flow of young women, many of them minors or barely of legal age, through his modelling operations; Epstein provided financial backing, social capital, and a market — in the most grotesque sense — for the women Brunel supplied.

## II. MC2 MODEL MANAGEMENT

### Founding

MC2 Model Management was founded in **2005** and based in **Miami, Florida**, with operations extending to New York and Tel Aviv. The agency was registered under Brunel's name and presented itself as a legitimate boutique modelling agency specializing in the discovery and placement of new talent, particularly from Eastern Europe, Brazil, and France (Miami Herald, 2018; Brown, 2019). [CONFIRMED]

The timing of MC2's founding is significant. It was established in the same year that the Palm Beach Police Department opened its investigation into Jeffrey Epstein for sexual offenses against minors. Whether this timing was coincidental or reflected an effort to formalize and professionalize a recruitment pipeline that had previously operated more informally remains a matter of investigative interest. [UNVERIFIED — temporal correlation only]

### Epstein as Financial Backer

**Jeffrey Epstein was a documented financial backer of MC2 Model Management.** This fact is not a matter of allegation; it was established through:

- **Maxwell Trial Evidence (2021)**: Trial Exhibit GX-351, admitted during the United States v. Ghislaine Maxwell trial, documented financial transfers from Epstein-controlled entities to Brunel and MC2 (United States v. Maxwell, 20-cr-00330, SDNY, 2021). [CONFIRMED]

- **Civil Proceedings**: Multiple civil lawsuits filed by Epstein victims referenced Epstein's financial relationship with MC2. Court filings in the Southern District of New York and the Southern District of Florida documented wire transfers, lease payments, and other financial support flowing from Epstein to MC2 and to Brunel personally (Giuffre v. Maxwell, 15-cv-07433, SDNY; Edwards & Cassell filings, SDFL). [CONFIRMED]

- **Financial Records**: Banking records obtained by investigators showed that Epstein provided at least **$1 million** in seed funding for MC2, and continued to make periodic payments to the agency and to Brunel throughout the agency's operation. Some transfers were characterized as "loans" or "investments," but no repayment schedule or equity arrangement consistent with a legitimate business investment was documented (Brown, 2019; Miami Herald, 2018). [CORROBORATED]

The financial backing gave Epstein a direct structural interest in MC2's operations and, crucially, access to the models it recruited. Epstein was not a passive investor; testimony and documentary evidence indicate he was directly involved in reviewing model portfolios and in receiving introductions to MC2 models (Giuffre Deposition, 2016). [CORROBORATED]

### The Business Model

MC2's ostensible business model was straightforward and, on its surface, unremarkable for a boutique modelling agency:

1. **Scouting**: Brunel and MC2 scouts identified young women in their home countries — primarily Brazil, France, the former Soviet Union and Eastern European nations (particularly Russia, Ukraine, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic), and Scandinavia — who possessed the physical attributes valued by the commercial fashion industry.

2. **Visa Sponsorship**: MC2 sponsored models for entry into the United States, typically on H-1B (specialty occupation) or O-1 (extraordinary ability) or P-1 visas, though many models entered on tourist or student visas and were then employed by MC2 in potential violation of their visa terms. The agency handled visa paperwork, housing, and initial placement (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

3. **Housing**: MC2 provided or arranged shared housing for models in Miami. Models were typically placed in agency-controlled apartments, with rent deducted from their future earnings — a standard practice in the modelling industry that also created a debt relationship and physical dependence on the agency (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

4. **Placement**: MC2 booked models for fashion shows, commercial shoots, catalogue work, and private events. A portion of these placements were legitimate; others were pretextual introductions to Epstein and his associates. [CORROBORATED]

### Structural Trafficking Vulnerability

The combination of visa sponsorship, housing provision, financial dependence, and geographic isolation created what trafficking scholars describe as a near-textbook set of **coercive control mechanisms**:

- **Visa dependence**: A model whose legal right to remain in the United States was tied to her agency's sponsorship could not leave the agency, refuse assignments, or report abuse without risking deportation. This created a powerful disincentive to complain and a powerful incentive to comply with agency demands, including demands that went beyond legitimate modelling work (Polaris Project, 2018). [CORROBORATED]

- **Financial dependence**: Models who had been advanced rent, airfare, and living expenses by MC2 arrived in the United States already in debt to the agency. With earnings controlled by the agency and deductions taken before payment, many models had little or no independent financial resources. This economic dependence mirrors the debt bondage mechanisms documented in other trafficking contexts (U.S. Department of State, Trafficking in Persons Report, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

- **Geographic and linguistic isolation**: Young women recruited from Eastern Europe, Brazil, or France were typically in an unfamiliar country, often with limited English, far from family and friends, and wholly reliant on the agency for social as well as professional connections. This isolation made them extraordinarily vulnerable to exploitation (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

- **Industry normalization**: The modelling industry's culture — in which young women are expected to attend parties, socialize with wealthy patrons, and maintain a network of powerful contacts — provided a veneer of normalcy for introductions to men like Epstein. The line between a "networking event" and a trafficking transaction was deliberately blurred (Gross, 1995; Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

### The "Booker" Role

Within MC2, the role of "booker" — the staff member responsible for scheduling models' appointments and managing their calendars — was key to the trafficking pipeline. Bookers at MC2 were responsible for:

- Scheduling models to attend events at Epstein's residences (primarily the Palm Beach estate and the New York townhouse)
- Framing these events as networking opportunities, parties, or social engagements beneficial to the model's career
- In some cases, specifically directing models to provide "massages" to Epstein — the euphemism documented extensively in the Palm Beach Police investigation and subsequent federal proceedings (Recarey Police Report, 2005; Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

The booker function was the operational link between the legitimate-seeming agency and the criminal enterprise. It was through booker-mediated scheduling that models were delivered to Epstein.

## III. THE TRAFFICKING PIPELINE — OPERATIONAL MECHANICS

### Recruitment in Eastern Europe, Brazil, and France

Brunel's recruitment operations were geographically diverse but concentrated in regions where economic conditions, limited opportunities for young women, and weak regulatory oversight of the modelling industry created optimal conditions for exploitation:

- **Eastern Europe (Russia, Ukraine, Slovakia, Czech Republic)**: The collapse of the Soviet Union and the economic upheaval of the 1990s and 2000s created a large pool of young women for whom a modelling career in the West represented an almost unimaginable opportunity. Brunel and MC2 scouts operated in these countries, attending local beauty contests, visiting modelling schools, and approaching young women and their families with promises of careers in Miami, New York, and Paris (Brown, 2019; Le Monde, 2020). [CORROBORATED]

- **Brazil**: Brunel had long-standing recruitment operations in Brazil, dating to his Karin Models and Europlanning days. Brazilian models were recruited through similar channels — local agencies, beauty pageants, and direct scouting — and brought to the United States on MC2 sponsorship (Gross, 1995; Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

- **France**: Brunel's home base. French models were recruited through Parisian agencies and scouting networks, with MC2 serving as the vehicle for their placement in the United States (Le Monde, 2020). [CORROBORATED]

### Visa Sponsorship as Control Mechanism

The visa mechanism deserves particular emphasis because it functioned as the single most effective coercive tool in the pipeline:

1. MC2 would identify a recruit and offer to sponsor her entry into the United States.
2. The agency would handle all paperwork, often with the model having little understanding of the specific visa category, its terms, or its restrictions.
3. Upon arrival, the model's legal status was entirely dependent on continued sponsorship by MC2. If the agency withdrew sponsorship — or simply failed to renew paperwork — the model became undocumented.
4. This dependency was understood and, in several documented cases, explicitly leveraged. Models who resisted unwanted introductions or who expressed discomfort with the nature of certain "bookings" were reminded of their precarious legal status (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

This mechanism is formally recognized by the U.S. Department of Justice as a trafficking indicator: the use of immigration status to coerce compliance with forced labor or sexual exploitation (DOJ, 2020).

### The "Introduction" to Epstein

The process by which MC2 models were introduced to Epstein followed a documented pattern:

1. **Initial framing**: Models were told they would be attending a party, dinner, or social event hosted by a wealthy and powerful man who could advance their careers. The event was presented as a professional opportunity — a chance to meet photographers, designers, or potential clients (Giuffre Deposition, 2016; Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

2. **Arrival**: Models were typically transported to Epstein's Palm Beach residence or New York townhouse. In some cases, they were flown to other locations, including the U.S. Virgin Islands (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

3. **The "massage" request**: Once at the residence, models were frequently asked to provide a "massage" to Epstein — the same pretext documented in scores of victim statements across the broader Epstein investigation. This request, which was presented as casual and normal, was the gateway to sexual abuse (Recarey Police Report, 2005; Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

4. **Escalation**: Depending on the victim's compliance, the encounter could escalate from a massage to sexual touching, oral sex, or rape. Victims who complied — whether through coercion, confusion, or fear — were often invited back. Those who resisted were typically not rebooked but were also, in many cases, not further supported by the agency (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

### The Sub-Recruitment Parallel

A particularly insidious feature of the Epstein-Brunel pipeline was the use of existing victims as recruiters for new victims — a pattern extensively documented in the broader Epstein investigation:

- Some models who had been introduced to Epstein through MC2 were subsequently encouraged (and in some cases paid) to bring additional young women to Epstein. This "pyramid" recruitment model — in which victims become recruiters — is a hallmark of sophisticated trafficking operations and served to insulate the principals (Epstein, Brunel, Maxwell) from direct recruitment activity (United States v. Maxwell, Trial Testimony, 2021). [CONFIRMED — trial testimony]

- The recruitment incentive was both financial (payments of $200–$300 per recruit, consistent with the broader Epstein pattern) and professional (continued agency support, better bookings, visa renewal) (Brown, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

### Maxwell's Interface with MC2

Ghislaine Maxwell's role as coordinator between Brunel/MC2 and Epstein was documented through multiple evidentiary channels:

- **Phone records**: Epstein's recovered phone logs show extensive communication between Maxwell and Brunel, often in clusters around periods when Epstein was at his Palm Beach or New York residences (Recarey Police Report, 2005). [CONFIRMED]

- **Giuffre testimony**: Virginia Giuffre testified in her deposition that Maxwell directly coordinated with Brunel regarding the provision of models/girls to Epstein, and that Maxwell sometimes traveled to meet Brunel in connection with these arrangements (Giuffre Deposition, 2016). [CORROBORATED — deposition testimony]

- **Maxwell trial evidence**: The prosecution in United States v. Maxwell presented evidence of Maxwell's awareness of and involvement in the Brunel-MC2 pipeline, though Brunel was not a defendant in that proceeding (United States v. Maxwell, 2021). [CONFIRMED]

Maxwell served as the organizational hub: she received requests from Epstein, communicated with Brunel regarding the availability and characteristics of models, and in some cases personally arranged logistics (flights, accommodation, scheduling) for models being brought to Epstein.

## V. THE FRENCH INVESTIGATION

### Timeline

- **2019**: Following the arrest of Jeffrey Epstein in July 2019 and the renewed global attention to his trafficking network, French authorities came under significant public and political pressure to investigate Brunel. The Paris prosecutor's office opened a preliminary inquiry in August 2019, initially focused on potential offenses committed on French soil or involving French nationals or residents (Le Monde, 2019). [CONFIRMED]

- **2019–2020**: The inquiry was assigned to the **JIRS (Juridiction interrégionale spécialisée)** in Paris — a specialized jurisdiction for complex organized crime and trafficking cases. Investigators took statements from multiple complainants, examined Brunel's travel records, financial transactions, and communications, and coordinated with U.S. authorities (Le Monde, 2020). [CORROBORATED]

- **October 2020**: The investigation was upgraded. French investigators issued a wanted notice for Brunel, who had been traveling between France, South America, and West Africa (Le Monde, 2020). [CORROBORATED]

### Charges

Brunel was investigated for and ultimately charged with:

- **Rape of a minor** (viol sur mineur) — relating to allegations involving victims who were under 18 at the time of the alleged offenses
- **Sexual harassment** (harcèlement sexuel)
- **Human trafficking for the purpose of sexual exploitation** (traite des êtres humains aux fins d'exploitation sexuelle) — charges that directly addressed the MC2 pipeline

These charges carried potential sentences of 20 years to life imprisonment under French law (Code pénal, Articles 222-23 to 222-26; Article 225-4-1). [CONFIRMED — French judicial filings, as reported by AFP and Le Monde]

### Arrest: December 16, 2020

On **December 16, 2020**, Jean-Luc Brunel was arrested by French police at **Charles de Gaulle Airport** in Paris. He was apprehended as he attempted to board a flight to **Dakar, Senegal**. Brunel had been under surveillance, and investigators timed the arrest to prevent his departure from French jurisdiction (AFP, 2020; BBC News, 2020). [CONFIRMED — Paris Prosecutor's Office statement]

The arrest was widely reported internationally and was seen as a significant development in the post-Epstein accountability effort. At the time of his arrest, Brunel was 74 years old.

### Detention at La Santé Prison

Following his arrest, Brunel was placed in **pretrial detention** at **La Santé Prison** in Paris's 14th arrondissement. La Santé is one of France's most historically significant prisons, having housed high-profile detainees for more than a century.

- Brunel's legal team repeatedly filed motions for release on bail or under judicial supervision (contrôle judiciaire), arguing that his age and health made pretrial detention disproportionate. Each motion was denied by the investigating magistrate (juge d'instruction), who cited flight risk and the severity of the charges (Le Monde, 2021). [CORROBORATED]

- Brunel was the sole defendant in the French proceedings. Ghislaine Maxwell, who was in U.S. custody, was not a co-defendant. No other individuals were formally charged in connection with the French investigation at the time of Brunel's death. [CONFIRMED]

## VI. DEATH AT LA SANTÉ — FEBRUARY 19, 2022

### Official Ruling

On **February 19, 2022**, Jean-Luc Brunel was found dead in his cell at La Santé Prison. He was discovered during a routine night check at approximately 1:00 a.m.

- **Cause of death**: Suicide by hanging, using bedsheets tied to a window bar. [CONFIRMED — French Ministry of Justice statement, February 19, 2022]

- **No signs of foul play** were identified by initial examination, according to the Paris prosecutor's office (AFP, 2022). [CONFIRMED — official statement]

- **No independent autopsy** was commissioned by Brunel's family or legal team. The standard French medicolegal examination was conducted (Le Monde, 2022). [CONFIRMED]

### The Epstein Parallel

The circumstances of Brunel's death prompted immediate and widespread commentary on the parallels with Jeffrey Epstein's death:

| Factor | Epstein (Aug. 10, 2019) | Brunel (Feb. 19, 2022) |
|---|---|---|
| Facility | MCC New York | La Santé, Paris |
| Status | Pretrial detainee | Pretrial detainee |
| Method | Hanging (bedsheet) | Hanging (bedsheet) |
| Independent autopsy | Commissioned by family (Dr. Baden) — disputed findings | None commissioned |
| Surveillance | Camera malfunction documented | No public disclosure of surveillance status |
| Key witness status | Central figure; held information on co-conspirators | Key figure; held information on Epstein network, modelling industry, and French victims |
| Prosecution impact | Case extinguished | French case extinguished against Brunel |

The parallel is, at minimum, deeply unfortunate for the pursuit of justice. Both men were the possessors of information that could have implicated additional individuals in the trafficking network. Both died before trial. Both deaths were officially ruled suicides. [CONFIRMED — official rulings in both cases]

It must be stated clearly: **there is no confirmed evidence that either death was anything other than suicide.** The official findings in both cases remain the operative conclusions. However, the pattern has generated legitimate concern among victims' advocates, legal commentators, and investigators about the adequacy of pretrial custody safeguards for high-profile detainees in trafficking cases. [CORROBORATED — public commentary by victims' attorneys and French judicial union representatives]

### Testimony Permanently Lost

Brunel's death extinguished the possibility of the following testimony, which can now never be obtained:

1. **Firsthand account of Epstein's direct involvement in MC2 operations**: Brunel was the only individual with direct, personal knowledge of the scope and nature of Epstein's control over MC2, including specific instructions regarding the recruitment and provision of models/victims.

2. **Identification of additional network members**: Brunel operated across multiple countries and decades. His testimony could have identified scouts, bookers, facilitators, and clients who participated in or benefited from the pipeline but whose names do not appear in currently available evidence.

3. **French and European dimensions of the network**: The French investigation was specifically focused on offenses committed in France and involving European victims — a dimension of the Epstein network that remains substantially less documented than its U.S. operations.

4. **The role of other prominent figures**: Brunel's social and professional networks intersected with numerous wealthy, powerful, and publicly known individuals. His testimony under oath could have clarified the nature and extent of these individuals' knowledge of and involvement in the trafficking enterprise. [CORROBORATED — assessment based on known evidentiary record]

### Post-Death Status of the French Investigation

Following Brunel's death, the French investigation against him was formally closed, as French law does not permit posthumous prosecution. However:

- The Paris JIRS announced that the broader investigation into Epstein-linked offenses on French soil would continue, with a focus on potential accomplices and other individuals who may have facilitated trafficking or committed sexual offenses in connection with the network (AFP, 2022). [CONFIRMED]

- As of this writing (February 2026), no additional indictments have been publicly announced in connection with the French investigation. Victims' advocates have criticized the pace and scope of the French proceedings (Le Monde, 2023). [CORROBORATED]

## VII. THE COVER-UP DIMENSION

### Prior Knowledge by Modelling Industry Insiders

The documentary record makes clear that Brunel's predatory behavior was widely known within the international modelling industry long before his connection to Epstein became public:

- **The 1988** ***60 Minutes*** **broadcast** reached millions of viewers and specifically named Brunel. The modelling industry's response was effectively nil: no agency severed ties with Brunel, no industry body censured him, and no formal investigation was initiated by any industry self-regulatory organization (CBS News, 1988; Gross, 1995). [CONFIRMED — broadcast record]

- **Michael Gross's reporting (1995)** documented that complaints about Brunel were so common within the industry that they constituted open knowledge. Gross quoted industry figures who acknowledged Brunel's reputation but described it as an accepted feature of the business (Gross, 1995). [CORROBORATED]

- **Post-2019 statements**: Following Epstein's arrest and Brunel's public identification as a co-conspirator, multiple former models, agents, and industry professionals stated publicly that Brunel's behavior had been known for decades and that the industry had protected him through a combination of silence, financial incentive, and institutional indifference (BBC News, 2020; New York Times, 2019). [CORROBORATED]

### Elite Modelling World's Protection

The structural protection extended to Brunel illustrates a broader pattern within the fashion and modelling industries:

- **No industry blacklist**: Despite public allegations spanning decades, Brunel was never placed on
