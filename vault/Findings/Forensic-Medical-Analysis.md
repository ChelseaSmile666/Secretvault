## 1. Prefatory Clinical Note

The death of Jeffrey Edward Epstein on August 10, 2019, at the Metropolitan Correctional Center (MCC), New York, presents one of the most forensically contested custodial death cases in modern American legal history. Two qualified forensic pathologists — one acting as New York City's Chief Medical Examiner, one serving as an independent expert engaged by the decedent's family — examined identical physical evidence and reached materially different professional conclusions. This divergence is not attributable to incompetence on either side; rather, it reflects a genuine evidentiary ambiguity that the available physical record cannot fully resolve.

This analysis proceeds from first principles of forensic pathology. The discipline of forensic pathology concerns itself with the determination of cause of death (the disease or injury responsible for the fatal physiological cascade), mechanism of death (the pathophysiological process by which the cause produced death), and manner of death (the classification — natural, accidental, homicide, suicide, or undetermined). It is the last of these — manner — that is contested in the Epstein case.

A critical distinction must be stated at the outset: the **physical findings** of hanging and homicidal strangulation can overlap substantially, particularly in full-suspension versus partial-suspension hanging, and in manual versus ligature strangulation. The forensic literature does not support a claim that any single physical finding is pathognomonic (exclusively diagnostic) for either mechanism. The differential is probabilistic, not binary (DiMaio & DiMaio, 2001; Dolinak et al., 2005; Spitz, 2006).

## 6. Clinical Analysis: The Hyoid Bone Evidence

### Anatomical Background

The hyoid bone is a U-shaped structure at the base of the tongue, suspended by musculature and ligaments from the styloid processes of the skull. It is the only bone in the human body without articulation to any other bone, held entirely by soft tissue. It functions to anchor the tongue musculature and facilitate swallowing and phonation.

The hyoid ossifies progressively through life. In children and young adults, the greater horns are typically cartilaginous and flexible. By the sixth decade of life, the horns are typically fully ossified, rendering them more brittle and susceptible to fracture under compressive or tensile loading (Pollanen & Chiasson, 1996).

### Fracture Mechanics

The hyoid bone can fracture under two distinct biomechanical conditions relevant to forensic death investigation:

**In hanging**: The ligature applies force superior and lateral to the hyoid, pulling the laryngeal complex superiorly and compressing it against the base of skull. The direction of force is predominantly vertical and anterior. Hyoid fractures in hanging tend to result from indirect tensile stress as the laryngeal structure is displaced upward. The literature suggests this more typically produces fracture of the body or isolated horn fractures (Maxeiner, 1998).

**In manual or ligature strangulation**: The hyoid is compressed directly, antero-posteriorly or laterally, by external hand pressure or a horizontally-applied ligature. This direct compression produces fractures more commonly at the greater horns, bilaterally, and may also fracture the thyroid cartilage horns (Luke et al., 1985; Maxeiner, 1998).

### Published Frequency Data

The forensic literature provides the following relevant statistics on hyoid fractures by mechanism:

**Hanging (all ages)**:
- Hyoid fracture frequency: approximately 17–27% of hanging deaths
- Frequency increases with age: in decedents over 60, estimated 26–34%
- Bilateral horn fractures: less commonly documented than unilateral; data limited
(Pollanen & Chiasson, 1996; Nikolic et al., 2000; Yen et al., 1994)

**Strangulation (manual and ligature)**:
- Hyoid fracture frequency: approximately 26–36% of strangulation deaths
- Manual strangulation produces hyoid fractures at higher rates than ligature strangulation
- Bilateral greater horn fractures are more commonly described in strangulation case series
(Luke et al., 1985; DiMaio & DiMaio, 2001; Maxeiner, 1998)

**Critical finding**: The key statistical point is not simply that hyoid fractures occur in both mechanisms — they do. The evidentiary weight lies in the **bilateral** nature of the hyoid horn fractures documented by Baden, combined with the **thyroid cartilage fracture**. The simultaneous presence of these three fractures (bilateral greater horns + superior thyroid horn) constitutes a more severe injury complex than is typically described in hanging case series, and appears more frequently in strangulation case series, though the literature does not provide a clean exclusionary boundary.

The most rigorous published study on this distinction — Nikolic et al. (2000) — examined 230 hanging deaths and found hyoid fractures in 27%, with bilateral fractures in only a subset of those; comparatively, in a strangulation series, bilateral hyoid fractures were substantially more common. The senior forensic textbook by Spitz (2006) states: "Multiple hyoid fractures, particularly bilateral greater horn fractures combined with thyroid cartilage fractures, are more characteristic of vigorous external neck compression than of hanging" (p. 876).

### Assessment

**[DISPUTED BY INDEPENDENT AUTOPSY]**

The hyoid bone evidence constitutes the strongest physical basis for Baden's alternative hypothesis. The official ME's characterisation — that such injuries are "not unusual" in older hanging victims — is technically accurate at the level of hyoid fracture in general, but the specific pattern of bilateral greater horn fractures combined with thyroid cartilage injury represents a more unusual constellation for a hanging death. The literature does not categorically exclude hanging as a cause; however, it does assign higher prior probability to strangulation for this specific injury pattern, particularly in the absence of long-drop or high-force hanging (Maxeiner, 1998; DiMaio & DiMaio, 2001).

At the MCC, the cell bunk bed height (addressed in Section 9) would not have permitted a long-drop hanging, meaning the mechanism would have been low-suspension or partial-suspension hanging with a body weight load — a mechanism associated with lower cervical forces than full-drop hanging, making the bilateral tri-fracture pattern more biomechanically unusual.

## 7. Clinical Analysis: Ligature Mark Characteristics

**[INCONCLUSIVE]**

### Differential Diagnosis of Ligature Furrow Characteristics

The morphology of the ligature mark on the neck is the single most diagnostically important surface finding in distinguishing hanging from strangulation. The classic discriminators are:

```
LIGATURE MARK MORPHOLOGY
=========================

HANGING (typical):
  Direction:    Oblique — angles upward toward point of suspension
  Pattern:      Inverted V / V-shape when viewed anteriorly
  Depth:        Deepest at lateral aspects, lightens at nape
  Continuity:   Often interrupted at suspension point (gap posteriorly)
  Width:        Corresponds to ligature width

  Side view:
       [Nape of neck — gap or shallow]
      /                               \
  [Ascending]                     [Ascending]
      \                               /
       [Deepest mark — lateral/anterior]


LIGATURE STRANGULATION (typical):
  Direction:    Horizontal — runs parallel to ground
  Pattern:      Complete circumferential groove
  Depth:        Relatively uniform around circumference
  Continuity:   Usually complete (no gap)
  Width:        Corresponds to ligature width

  Side view:
  --------[uniform horizontal furrow]---------
```

### Epstein's Ligature Mark — Reported Characteristics

Based on reporting from both the ME findings and Baden's observations, the ligature mark on Epstein's neck was described as **oblique** in orientation, which is the pattern associated with hanging rather than strangulation. This is a significant point in favour of the official suicide ruling.

However, Baden and other forensic commentators noted the following qualifications:

1. An oblique mark can be produced in a staged hanging if the body is repositioned or if a partial-suspension position is used
2. The depth and uniformity of the furrow in partial-suspension hanging may differ from expected hanging patterns
3. The specific level on the neck where the ligature mark was located — higher or lower — has diagnostic implications. A mark at or above the thyroid cartilage is more consistent with strangulation; below the thyroid is less discriminatory

The bedsheet reportedly used as the ligature was described in press accounts as a standard institutional sheet; the width of the ligature mark would correspond to the folded or knotted configuration. Standard bedsheets produce wider furrow marks than cords, which can complicate interpretation.

**Assessment**: The oblique ligature mark morphology is the finding most supportive of the official hanging/suicide ruling. It represents genuine physical evidence against the strangulation hypothesis. Baden acknowledged this finding but argued the other evidence (hyoid complex, internal hemorrhage) overweighed it.

## 8. Clinical Analysis: Petechial Hemorrhage

**[INCONCLUSIVE]**

### Pathophysiology

Petechiae (singular: petechia) are pinpoint hemorrhages, typically 1–3 mm in diameter, resulting from rupture of small capillaries and venules secondary to a sharp transient increase in intravascular pressure. In the context of asphyxial death, they arise because:

1. Sudden venous obstruction (from neck compression) impedes drainage from the head
2. Arterial flow continues momentarily, increasing capillary pressure
3. Capillary walls rupture, producing the characteristic punctate hemorrhages
4. They are most visible in the conjunctivae (inner eyelid), facial skin, and sclera

Petechiae are reliably produced in asphyxial deaths and are expected findings in both hanging and strangulation.

### Forensic Discriminating Value

The **presence** of petechiae in an asphyxial death carries minimal discriminatory value between hanging and strangulation — they occur in both. The more forensically relevant variables are:

- **Distribution**: Facial petechiae tend to be more extensive in strangulation because the obstruction is more complete and sustained; in hanging, the level of venous obstruction may be less consistent depending on suspension position
- **Bilaterality of conjunctival involvement**: Bilateral dense conjunctival petechiae with facial spread suggests more sustained and complete venous obstruction, more typical of strangulation or prolonged partial-suspension hanging
- **Density**: Dense, confluent petechiae (approaching "petechial storm") are more commonly described in manual strangulation

Baden noted the presence of petechiae but their evidential weight in distinguishing the two mechanisms is genuinely low in this case. The official ME also noted petechiae and attributed them to the hanging mechanism.

**Assessment**: The petechiae are a confirmed finding consistent with asphyxial death by either mechanism. They do not discriminate reliably between hanging and strangulation in this case. **[INCONCLUSIVE]**

## 9. Biomechanical Assessment: MCC Cell Dimensions and Hanging Mechanics

**[INCONCLUSIVE]**

### Physical Requirements for Hanging

For hanging to produce effective asphyxiation, sufficient force must be applied to the neck to obstruct the airway and/or carotid and vertebral blood flow. This does not require complete body suspension. Studies have demonstrated that as little as 5 kg (approximately 11 lbs) of force — well below full body weight — applied to the anterior neck is sufficient to obstruct venous drainage; approximately 15–22 kg is required for carotid arterial obstruction; tracheal occlusion requires higher forces but may not be the primary mechanism (Bell et al., 1992; Reay & Eisele, 1982).

**Partial-suspension hanging** — where the feet remain in contact with the ground or the body is in a semi-prone or kneeling position — is fully capable of producing fatal asphyxia and is well-documented in forensic case series. It accounts for a significant proportion of custodial hanging deaths specifically (Fruehwald et al., 2004).

### MCC Cell Physical Parameters

Based on publicly reported information about the MCC special housing unit cells:

| Parameter | Reported / Estimated |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Standard bunk bed height (upper bunk level) | Approximately 5–6 feet (1.5–1.8 m) from floor |
| Standard ceiling height, MCC cells | Approximately 9 feet (2.7 m) |
| Reported ligature attachment point | Upper bunk |
| Decedent height | Approximately 6 feet (1.83 m) |

If Epstein was 6 feet tall and the upper bunk was approximately 5.5 feet high, a ligature attached to the bunk framework could produce a partial-suspension hanging with feet on the floor or knees on the floor. This is mechanically feasible for a fatal hanging. A full-suspension hanging from the bunk would require the decedent to elevate himself above the bunk level, which would require active effort or another attachment point.

**Significance for injury pattern**: Partial-suspension or low-attachment-point hanging produces lower neck-compression forces compared to judicial-style long-drop hanging. This is relevant because the more severe tri-fracture hyoid-laryngeal complex injuries documented by Baden are more commonly associated with either high-force hanging or with external neck compression (strangulation). A low-force partial-suspension hanging is mechanically less likely to produce bilateral hyoid horn fractures, though it remains within the range of published cases (Maxeiner, 1998).

**Assessment**: The MCC cell dimensions are consistent with a partial-suspension hanging being physically possible. However, the relatively low forces expected from partial-suspension hanging at this bunk height are less congruent with the extensive hyoid-laryngeal fracture complex documented. This represents a biomechanical tension within the official ruling. **[INCONCLUSIVE]**

## 10. The July 23, 2019 Neck Injury — The Tartaglione Incident

**[INCONCLUSIVE]**

### Documented Events

On July 23, 2019 — 18 days before Epstein's death — Epstein was found semi-conscious in his cell at MCC with marks on his neck. He was treated and returned to housing after a brief period on suicide watch.

His cellmate at the time was Nicholas Tartaglione, a former police officer awaiting trial for quadruple murder. Tartaglione's attorney publicly stated that Tartaglione denied harming Epstein and claimed Epstein's injuries were self-inflicted (a staged suicide attempt to obtain a transfer to better conditions).

### Medical Significance

The July 23 incident has three significant forensic implications for the August 10 death:

**First**, any residual soft-tissue injury or subclinical fracture of the hyoid-laryngeal complex from the July 23 incident could influence the interpretation of findings at autopsy. A previously injured hyoid horn is more susceptible to re-fracture under lower forces. If Epstein sustained an assault on July 23 that produced partial or hairline hyoid injuries, those structures would be weakened for any subsequent event — whether a hanging suicide attempt or a strangulation homicide.

**Second**, the July 23 incident establishes that Epstein had at least one episode of documented neck trauma and neck marks in the weeks preceding his death. This complicates clean differential diagnosis of the August 10 injuries.

**Third**, forensically: if the July 23 event was a genuine assault by Tartaglione (which Baden and Epstein's family have suggested), it demonstrates that Epstein faced physical violence in custody. The independent characterisation of that event influences prior probability assessments for the August 10 death mechanism.

The documentation of the July 23 injuries was reportedly documented in the MCC medical records, which have not been fully released publicly.

**Assessment**: The July 23 incident creates genuine forensic ambiguity. Pre-existing neck trauma in the hyoid-laryngeal region cannot be excluded as a contributing factor to the injury severity documented at the August 10 autopsy. A definitive forensic assessment would require direct comparison of radiological imaging from July 23 and histological aging of the August 10 fracture lines (see Section 16). **[INCONCLUSIVE]**

1. There was a prior documented interest in scopolamine in Epstein's own communications (EFTA00008744)
2. Victim testimony described chemical incapacitation consistent with anticholinergic effects
3. The forensic literature documents that scopolamine has a narrow detection window (see Section 13)

The absence of these compounds in the official toxicology report cannot be interpreted as evidence that they were absent at the time of death without knowledge of whether targeted testing was performed.

## 12. Pharmacological Analysis: Scopolamine in the Trafficking Operation

**[INCONCLUSIVE — documented interest confirmed; use on victims alleged not forensically confirmed]**

### Background and Source

Scopolamine (hyoscine) is a tropane alkaloid derived primarily from plants of the Solanaceae family, principally *Hyoscyamus niger*, *Datura stramonium* (jimson weed), *Brugmansia* species (angel's trumpet / "trumpet plants"), and related species. It is a competitive antagonist at muscarinic acetylcholine receptors throughout the central and peripheral nervous system.

### Documented Evidence of Epstein's Interest

DOJ file EFTA00008744, publicly released at justice.gov/epstein as part of the January 2026 document release, documents email communications from 2014–2015 in which Epstein received and apparently engaged with materials specifically describing scopolamine's properties. The subject line of forwarded email material referenced in reporting describes scopolamine as a drug that "eliminates free will."

Additional context from the same document set:
- References to "trumpet plants" in connection with nursery operations at Epstein properties align with *Brugmansia* species, which are the primary natural source of scopolamine outside pharmaceutical synthesis
- The explicit framing in forwarded emails concerns the drug's utility for producing states of compliance and amnesia — not its medical applications

### Mechanism of Action: CNS and Autonomic Effects

```
SCOPOLAMINE (HYOSCINE) — RECEPTOR PHARMACOLOGY
================================================

Target receptor:  Muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (M1–M5)
Mechanism:        Competitive antagonism
Primary CNS loci: Cortex, hippocampus, limbic system, reticular activating system

                    [CORTEX]
                      |
              [HIPPOCAMPUS] ← memory encoding suppressed
                      |
           [LIMBIC SYSTEM] ← emotional regulation impaired
                      |
   [RETICULAR ACTIVATING] ← sedation / altered consciousness
          SYSTEM

Peripheral effects (anticholinergic):
  - Pupil dilation (mydriasis)
  - Dry mouth
  - Tachycardia
  - Urinary retention
  - Decreased secretions
```

### Graded Dose-Effect Relationship

| Dose Range | Route | Primary Effects | Forensic Relevance |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 0.1–0.3 mg | Oral / transdermal | Mild sedation, mild amnesia | Sub-threshold for incapacitation; detectable |
| 0.3–0.6 mg | Oral | Moderate sedation, anterograde amnesia, suggestibility | Primary "compliance dose" — victim functional but amnestic |
| 0.6–1.0 mg | Oral | Significant sedation, confusion, anterograde + partial retrograde amnesia, hallucinations | Victim may appear intoxicated |
| > 1.0 mg | Oral | Delirium, extreme agitation or stupor, risk of hyperthermia, respiratory depression | Overdose territory; more readily detected |

The forensically relevant dose for covert criminal use is the intermediate range (0.3–0.6 mg), producing a cooperative, amnestic state without obvious external incapacitation. This is consistent with victim testimony describing feeling "loopy" and experiencing memory gaps without full loss of consciousness.

**Key forensic conclusion on scopolamine detection**: The narrow detection window (blood: 12–24 hours; urine: 24–72 hours) means that a forensic toxicology examination performed on a victim who reports or is discovered more than 24–48 hours after ingestion will almost invariably return a negative result on standard panels, even if scopolamine was present at the time of the incident. Hair follicle analysis extending back through the relevant period represents the only retrospective detection method with any reliability beyond 72 hours (Scott-Ham & Burton, 2005; Flanagan et al., 2007; Mieczkowski & Newel, 2000).

## 15. Synthesis: What Does the Totality of Evidence Suggest?

This section represents the professional forensic synthesis — the clinical opinion that emerges from weighing all documented physical evidence.

### Evidence Summary Matrix

| Evidence Item | Supports Suicide | Supports Strangulation | Weight |
|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|--------|
| Oblique ligature furrow | Yes — strongly | No | High |
| Bilateral hyoid horn fractures | Weakly (occurs in hanging) | Yes — more common | High |
| Thyroid cartilage fracture | Weakly (occurs in older adults in hanging) | Yes — more typical | Medium-High |
| Tri-fracture combination | Weakly (unusual for hanging) | Yes — more common combination | High |

| Prior neck injury July 23 | Ambiguous — could predispose either mechanism | Ambiguous | Low-Medium |
| Rapid removal from suicide watch | No pathological weight | Contextual | Contextual |

### Professional Clinical Assessment

A forensic pathologist reviewing this evidence must acknowledge the following:

**In favour of the official suicide ruling**:
The oblique ligature furrow morphology is genuine physical evidence that is characteristic of hanging and difficult to replicate in a homicidal strangulation scenario. Dr. Sampson's position — that the hyoid-laryngeal fractures, while severe, fall within the documented range for older-adult hanging deaths — is technically defensible. The absence of definitive evidence of a third party's presence (no DNA under fingernails, no defensive wounds, no cell intrusion documented) supports the official ruling.

**In favour of the strangulation hypothesis**:
The bilateral tri-fracture of the hyoid-laryngeal complex is, per the published forensic literature, an atypical injury pattern for partial-suspension hanging at the biomechanical forces available in the MCC cell. The constellation of bilateral greater horn hyoid fractures plus thyroid cartilage fracture is more commonly described in strangulation case series. Dr. Baden's experience — including serving as Chief Medical Examiner of New York City and having performed over 20,000 autopsies — lends significant professional weight to his alternative interpretation.

**The honest forensic position**:
The correct classification, given the available evidence, is arguably **undetermined** — which is the classification Baden himself preferred. "Undetermined" is not a finding of homicide; it is a finding that the physical evidence does not allow definitive assignment of manner of death. The NYC ME's choice of "suicide" as the final ruling is a defensible professional decision, but it represents a conclusion that goes beyond what the physical evidence strictly supports, in that it resolves ambiguity in a particular direction without the corroborating evidence (camera footage, toxicology for all relevant substances, the full forensic imaging record) that would ordinarily underpin certainty.

The confluence of documented institutional failures — non-operational cameras, unsupervised three-hour monitoring gap, removal of cellmate, premature removal from suicide watch, prior undetermined neck trauma — constitutes an extraordinary accumulation of anomalies. While each individual failure has a plausible routine explanation, their simultaneous occurrence on the same night in a case involving a defendant with connections to multiple powerful individuals represents a circumstantial context that a forensic investigation cannot scientifically quantify but must note.

**Bottom line clinical opinion**:

> Based on the physical evidence as publicly documented, the manner of death in the Epstein case cannot be determined with forensic certainty from the available record. The injury pattern — particularly the bilateral hyoid-laryngeal tri-fracture complex — is more consistent with external neck compression than with the partial-suspension hanging mechanism available in the MCC cell. The oblique ligature mark is the primary physical finding supporting the official hanging/suicide classification. In the presence of this genuine equipoise in the physical evidence, and in the absence of camera footage, targeted toxicology for relevant substances, and reliable witness documentation of the relevant hours, the most forensically defensible classification is "undetermined" rather than "suicide." The official ruling represents a legitimate professional judgment, but it is not the only legitimate professional judgment the evidence supports.

## 16. What Would Be Required for Definitive Forensic Determination

For a forensic pathologist to offer an opinion approaching certainty rather than probability, the following would be required:

### Physical Evidence Requirements

| Evidence Item | Status | Forensic Value |
|---------------|--------|----------------|
| Full unredacted autopsy report with photographs | Partially released (FOIA) | Would allow full review of ligature mark morphology and hemorrhage distribution |
| Radiological imaging (X-ray / CT) of hyoid-laryngeal complex | Not in public record | Would allow three-dimensional fracture characterisation and aging of fractures |
| Histological sections of fracture sites | Unknown if performed | Fracture age can be estimated by acute vs. chronic inflammatory response at fracture margins |
| Histological aging of fractures (July 23 vs. Aug 10) | Unknown if performed | Could determine whether injuries were from prior incident or acute event |
| Targeted toxicology (LC-MS/MS for tropane alkaloids, GHB, benzodiazepines) | Not performed or not released | Would address pharmacological incapacitation hypothesis |
| Hair follicle toxicology (scopolamine, GHB) | Not reported as performed | Only method for retrospective detection beyond 72 hours |
| Bedsheet physical analysis | Not fully public | Ligature width, knotting pattern, fiber transfer to neck would confirm or challenge hanging |
| DNA and fiber trace evidence from neck | Not in public record | Could establish third-party contact |
| Full functioning camera footage | Lost/non-functional | Would have been the definitive evidence |
| Medical records from July 23 incident | Not released | Critical for differential diagnosis of prior vs. acute hyoid injury |

### Statistical Framework

A formal Bayesian analysis of the physical evidence would require:

- Prior probability of custodial suicide at MCC (base rate for the relevant population)
- Likelihood ratio of the tri-fracture hyoid-laryngeal complex in hanging vs. strangulation (derivable from Nikolic et al., 2000; Maxeiner, 1998)
- Likelihood ratio of the oblique ligature mark in hanging vs. strangulation
- Combined posterior probability given all evidence

Without the additional forensic evidence listed above, the likelihood ratios cannot be calculated with sufficient precision to drive the posterior probability definitively toward either conclusion.
